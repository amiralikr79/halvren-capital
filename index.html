The Case for
Halvren Capital

Published April 2025
Halvren Capital Partners

To Our Future Partners

Most investment memos begin with a statement of edge. Ours begins with an admission: we don’t know what the market will do next quarter. Neither does anyone else, including the people who sound most certain about it. The hedge fund manager with the proprietary macro model, the sell-side analyst with the twelve-month price target, the talking head with seventeen minutes to fill — they are engaged in the respectable theater of precision. We are not.

What we do know — with the quiet confidence of someone who has read enough history to be chastened by it — is this: over sufficiently long periods, the ownership of truly excellent businesses is one of the most reliable ways to compound wealth that humanity has yet devised. Not the fastest. Not the most exciting. The most reliable.

“The stock market is a device for transferring money from the impatient to the patient. We intend to be on the right side of that transaction.”

Why We Exist

The financial industry is, at its core, a machine optimized for the production of activity. Activity generates fees. Fees fund offices in desirable postal codes, Bloomberg terminals, and the comfortable illusion that constant motion is the same thing as progress. We were built as a counterargument.

Halvren Capital was founded on the observation that most of what passes for sophisticated investing is elaborate noise reduction — and not particularly successful noise reduction at that. The average active fund underperforms its benchmark after fees. The average investor underperforms the average fund by panic-selling at the worst possible moment. The industry has industrialized the destruction of alpha.

We think there is a better way. Not a secret formula. Not a proprietary dataset that only we possess. Something more boring, and more durable: a genuine long-term orientation, applied consistently, by people who are temperamentally built for boredom and intellectually built for depth.

What We Actually Do

We read. We think. We wait. Occasionally — when all three activities converge on the same idea — we buy.

The businesses we seek to own share certain qualities that make them deeply unfashionable to discuss at cocktail parties. They tend to be comprehensible. They have economics that are almost embarrassingly straightforward: they earn more than they spend, the difference grows over time, and the reinvestment of that difference compounds at attractive rates. They have moats — not the imaginary moats that analysts construct from brand sentiment surveys, but the grinding, structural kind that take decades to build and cannot be replicated by a competitor with a large balance sheet and aggressive hiring.

We avoid businesses that require our continuous attention to remain excellent. If a company needs us watching it every day to behave itself, we don’t own enough of it to matter, and we own too much of it to sleep well.

On Risk

The financial industry has a peculiar relationship with risk. It measures it in standard deviations and betas — quantities that describe how a stock’s price has moved relative to other prices, which is not the same thing as risk in any sense that matters to a long-term owner of businesses.

We think about risk the way a structural engineer thinks about the load-bearing capacity of a bridge: not as the probability that something will fluctuate, but as the probability of permanent, unrecoverable loss. Volatility is not risk. Volatility is opportunity in uncomfortable clothing.

The second-order implication of this view is that we will, with some regularity, look foolish. Our portfolios will underperform during the phases of the cycle when the market rewards things we don’t own: the meme stocks, the narrative-driven SPACs, the crypto tokens that are indistinguishable from each other except in name. We accept this. The performance of a well-managed portfolio over a three-year period is interesting. Over a twenty-year period, it is conclusive.

“Every market cycle produces a new graveyard of people who had a unique edge. We plan to compound quietly in the background while that story plays out.”

On Technology and the Next Era

We are living through the most significant technological transition since electricity. The diffusion of general-purpose AI into the fabric of economic production will — over the next decade — restructure the cost basis of industries, eliminate the competitive advantage of scale in some sectors, and amplify it dramatically in others.

We hold a view that may be unfashionable in both the tech-skeptic and the tech-utopian camps: the primary beneficiaries of AI will not be the companies building AI, but the companies that know how to use it against incumbents who cannot. The electricity analogy is instructive. The fortunes of the twentieth century were not primarily made by electrical utility companies. They were made by the factories, retailers, and service businesses that rewired their operations first, fastest, and most completely.

We are looking, with considerable attention, for the factory owners of the current transition. They are, by definition, not obvious. Obvious opportunities are efficiently priced. We are in the business of the non-obvious, patiently discovered.

What We Ask of Our Partners

We ask for time. Not an unreasonable amount — a five-year horizon is sufficient to distinguish luck from skill in most investment contexts. A ten-year horizon is sufficient to make the distinction nearly certain.

We ask for calm during the periods when the market disagrees with us. It will, reliably, disagree with us. That is not a failure of our analysis; it is the mechanism by which our returns are generated. The spread between price and value is the engine of this strategy, and that spread widens most aggressively precisely when the psychological pressure to close it — by selling — is most intense.

In exchange, we commit to writing to you honestly. We will tell you when we are wrong, and why. We will tell you when we don’t know something, which will be more often than you might prefer. We will not produce a quarterly letter explaining how our portfolio, despite declining 14%, has in fact performed quite well given the circumstances. If we decline 14%, we declined 14%.

We will, eventually, write a memo about being wrong. We look forward to that memo. It means something interesting happened.

Core Beliefs

Five things we hold
to be self-evident

These are not marketing positions. They are the beliefs that determine what we own, what we ignore, and how we behave when markets make both feel equally unreasonable.

I.

Price is not value

The market is a voting machine in the short run and a weighing machine in the long run. We are in the weighing business. The gap between price and intrinsic value is not an anomaly to be explained away — it is the source of our returns, and it requires both patience and the occasional willingness to look stupid.

II.

Conviction is scarce

A portfolio of forty positions is not a portfolio. It is the financial equivalent of a restaurant menu that lists two hundred items. Real conviction — the kind that survives a 30% drawdown in a holding you’ve held for three years — is only available in approximately ten ideas at any given time. We allocate accordingly.

III.

Risk is permanent loss

Volatility is a feature, not a bug. The assets that fluctuate most dramatically in the short term are often the ones that compound most reliably over the long term — because the fluctuation creates the entry point. We distinguish between mark-to-market pain, which is temporary, and fundamental impairment, which is not.

IV.

Moats are earned, not declared

Every management team believes their company has a moat. The word appears with remarkable regularity in earnings calls, investor presentations, and letters to shareholders. We apply a simple test: if a well-capitalized, highly motivated competitor spent five years trying to take your customers, would you still have them? The honest answer is usually clarifying.

V.

The cycle always turns

Every era of market history ends with a consensus view that is not merely wrong but spectacularly, embarrassingly wrong — in ways that seem obvious in retrospect and genuinely invisible in the moment. The dot-com analyst who saw no ceiling, the 2007 mortgage underwriter who saw no floor. We manage with the quiet assumption that the current consensus is probably also, in some important respect, mistaken.

Investment Thesis

Where we look,
and why

Bucket One

Compounding
Machines

Businesses with pricing power, low capital requirements, and the structural ability to reinvest earnings at high rates of return for decades. These are the rare assets where the appropriate holding period is “until the thesis breaks, not until the price rises.” They tend to be boring. We are comfortable with boring.

Bucket Two

Dislocated
Quality

Excellent businesses temporarily mispriced due to macro noise, earnings seasonality, or the market’s irrational short-termism. The operative word is “temporarily.” We do not buy businesses that are cheap for genuine fundamental reasons and call it a dislocation. The distinction requires judgment. Judgment is our product.

Bucket Three

Technological
Transition Plays

Businesses in sectors undergoing AI-driven cost restructuring, where incumbents are too large and profitable to reinvent themselves and challengers have the advantage of building on the new cost basis from day one. We are not buying the shovels. We are looking for the miners who know where the ground is soft.

“We are looking for the factory owners of the current transition. They are, by definition, not obvious.”
