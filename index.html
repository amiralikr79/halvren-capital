Philosophy

Five beliefs we
won’t debate.

These are not investment principles we discovered in a textbook. They are things we got wrong — expensively — before understanding them as truths.

01

The price of admission to real returns is the discomfort of looking wrong for years. If your strategy feels comfortable to everyone in the room, it is not a strategy — it is a consensus.

02

We do not predict cycles. We position for the aftermath of other people’s predictions. Forecasters are not wrong because they are unintelligent. They are wrong because they are human.

03

A business compounding at 20% per year does not need our help. Our job is to not interfere. The hardest skill in investing is recognizing when the correct action is no action.

04

Technology does not disrupt industries. It reveals which operators were never actually good at them. We invest at the intersection of this revelation and the market’s delay in pricing it.

05

Risk is not volatility. Risk is permanent loss of capital. We are paid — eventually — to know the difference. Volatility is a feature. Permanent impairment is a failure of underwriting.

Investment Thesis

Where we look.
Why we wait.

We do not invest in ideas. We invest in businesses with durable competitive advantages, run by operators who think in decades, at prices that do not require optimism to justify.

Core Thesis

Compounders trading at prices that assume they will stop compounding.

The market’s structural bias toward the near-term creates persistent mispricings in businesses whose value is overwhelmingly long-duration. We are buyers when the consensus runs out of patience. We are sellers almost never.

“Price is what you pay. Value is what you get. We are in the business of finding large gaps between the two — and then waiting for the market to close them, whether or not we are watching.”

Bucket I

Capital-Light Businesses With Pricing Power

Software, marketplaces, and platforms where the incremental dollar of revenue costs nearly nothing to deliver and where switching costs compound with each passing year.

Bucket II

Industrials & Real Assets in Structural Undersupply

Hard assets, energy infrastructure, and industrial businesses where the replacement cost of capacity has decoupled from market pricing — and where the cycle will eventually correct both.

Bucket III

Emerging Market Operators With Local Moats

Dominant businesses in markets too small or too inconvenient for institutional capital. The MSCI index ignores them. That is precisely why we look.

What We Avoid

Businesses whose thesis requires the future to be predictable.

We have no view on where rates go, what inflation prints, or who wins the next election. We only buy businesses that would do well across a wide range of futures.

April 2025 Founding Memo

From the Desk of Halvren Capital

On why we are
starting this now.

We are not starting Halvren Capital because the market is cheap. It is not, particularly. We are not starting it because we have a unique edge that no one else has discovered. The graveyard of managers who opened with that sentence is long and well-populated.

We are starting it because we believe that the single greatest source of alpha available to a small, concentrated, long-duration investor is not information — it is temperament. And temperament, unlike information, cannot be replicated at scale.

“The question we ask before every investment is not ‘what is the upside?’ It is: ‘if we are wrong, how wrong can we be — and can we survive it?’ The superyacht can wait. Permanent capital cannot be rebuilt.”
— Halvren Capital Founding Memo, April 2025
